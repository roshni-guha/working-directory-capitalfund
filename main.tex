\section{Motivation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This model develops a reinforcement learning (RL) framework to dynamically allocate capital across global equity ETFs using macro and cross-asset signals.

The central observation is that equity markets in different regions --- U.S.\ (SPY), Japan (EWJ), India (INDA), China (MCHI), and the Eurozone (EZU) --- respond differently to the same macro conditions. A rising oil price may favour energy-exporting economies; rising volatility typically signals a flight to cash. Rather than encoding these relationships as hand-crafted rules, the model learns them from two decades of historical data through trial-and-error interaction with a simulated trading environment.

The benchmark is a simple equal-weight portfolio that allocates 16.67\% to each of the six tradeable assets and rebalances daily.

\section{Feature Engineering: Blended Momentum}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Motivation for Blending}

Raw momentum signals suffer from a well-known trade-off: short windows are noisy and prone to mean-reversion; long windows are stale and lag genuine trend changes. Rather than feeding the model multiple raw windows as independent features, we construct a single \textbf{blended momentum} signal (\texttt{bmom}) that weights four lookback horizons according to their expected informativeness.

\subsection{Definition}

For asset $i$ at time $t$, the $k$-day momentum is:
\[
m_k^{(i)} = \frac{P_t^{(i)} - P_{t-k}^{(i)}}{P_{t-k}^{(i)}}
\]

The blended momentum is the weighted sum across four horizons:
\[
\texttt{bmom}_t^{(i)}
= w_1 \cdot m_{10}^{(i)}
+ w_2 \cdot m_{20}^{(i)}
+ w_3 \cdot m_{40}^{(i)}
+ w_4 \cdot m_{60}^{(i)}
\]
with weights:
\[
[w_1,\; w_2,\; w_3,\; w_4] = [0.15,\; 0.35,\; 0.35,\; 0.15]
\]

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{cccl}
\toprule
Window & Weight & Contribution & Rationale \\
\midrule
10 days & 15\% & Recent (noisy)          & Captures momentum bursts; downweighted to reduce noise \\
20 days & 35\% & Short-to-medium trend   & Most persistent near-term directional signal \\
40 days & 35\% & Medium trend            & Captures regime persistence without excess lag \\
60 days & 15\% & Longer-term (stale)     & Provides context; downweighted to avoid staleness \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Blended momentum weight schedule. Center windows receive 70\% of total weight.}
\end{table}

The 15\,/\,35\,/\,35\,/\,15 schedule places most weight on the two center windows, damping both high-frequency noise and slow-moving long-term drift. This produces smoother regime signals that reduce unnecessary portfolio turnover.

\subsection{Impact on State Dimension}

Using \texttt{bmom} reduces the state dimension from 18 (6 signal ETFs $\times$ 3 raw windows) to 6 (6 signal ETFs $\times$ 1 blended feature), substantially lowering the risk of overfitting on a finite training set.

\section{Model Formulation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Markov Decision Process}

The trading problem is cast as a discrete-time MDP with:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State} $s_t \in \mathbb{R}^6$: blended momentum of the six macro signal ETFs.
    \item \textbf{Action} $a_t \in \{0,\ldots,5\}$: index of the ETF to hold at 100\% weight.
    \item \textbf{Reward} $r_t$: risk-adjusted daily return.
    \item \textbf{Horizon} $T$: full training period (approximately 4,700 days per episode).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{State Vector}

\[
s_t =
\bigl[\,
\texttt{bmom}_t^{(\text{TLT})},\;
\texttt{bmom}_t^{(\text{GLD})},\;
\texttt{bmom}_t^{(\text{USO})},\;
\texttt{bmom}_t^{(\text{SLV})},\;
\texttt{bmom}_t^{(\text{VIXY})},\;
\texttt{bmom}_t^{(\text{IBIT})}\,
\bigr]^\top
\]

Each dimension encodes the macro regime of a different cross-asset class:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TLT}: Risk-off sentiment and interest rate direction
    \item \textbf{GLD}: Inflation expectations and safe-haven demand
    \item \textbf{USO}: Global growth and energy cycle
    \item \textbf{SLV}: Industrial demand and commodity momentum
    \item \textbf{VIXY}: Equity volatility and fear gauge
    \item \textbf{IBIT}: Speculative risk appetite
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Action Space}

The agent selects one ETF to hold in full at each daily timestep:
\[
a_t \;\in\; \{\text{SPY-US},\; \text{EWJ-US},\; \text{INDA-US},\; \text{MCHI-US},\; \text{EZU-US},\; \text{BIL-US}\}
\]

Concentrating into a single position simplifies the credit assignment problem: the agent receives an unambiguous signal about whether each allocation decision was correct.

\subsection{Reward Function}

The reward balances daily return against realised volatility:
\[
r_t = R_t - \lambda \cdot \sigma_t, \qquad \lambda = 0.5
\]
where $R_t$ is the next-day return of the chosen ETF and $\sigma_t$ is its rolling 20-day standard deviation of returns. This Sharpe-like objective discourages the agent from holding high-return but highly volatile assets in unstable macro regimes.

\subsection{Objective}

The agent learns to maximise expected discounted cumulative reward:
\[
\pi^* = \arg\max_{\pi}\; \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t r_t\right], \qquad \gamma = 0.95
\]

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Neural Network Architecture}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The model maintains two separate networks following the actor-critic paradigm.

\subsection{Policy Network (Actor)}

The policy $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ maps the state to a probability distribution over actions:
\[
s \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{FC}_{128},\,\text{ReLU}\;}\;
\xrightarrow{\;\text{FC}_{128},\,\text{ReLU}\;}\;
\xrightarrow{\;\text{FC}_{6},\,\text{Softmax}\;}\;
P(a \mid s)
\]

Input dimension is 6 (\texttt{bmom} features); output is a 6-dimensional probability simplex over trade tickers. During training, actions are sampled from this distribution to encourage exploration. During inference, the argmax action is selected (pure exploitation).

\subsection{Value Network (Critic)}

The value function $V_\phi(s)$ estimates the expected future return from state $s$:
\[
s \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{FC}_{128},\,\text{ReLU}\;}\;
\xrightarrow{\;\text{FC}_{128},\,\text{ReLU}\;}\;
\xrightarrow{\;\text{FC}_{1}\;}\;
\hat{V}(s)
\]

The critic is used only during training to compute advantages and is discarded at inference time.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Training Algorithm}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Proximal Policy Optimization}

The model trains with PPO, which stabilises policy gradient updates by clipping the importance-sampling ratio. The PPO objective is:
\[
\mathcal{L}^{\text{PPO}}(\theta)
= \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[
\min\!\left(
\rho_t \hat{A}_t,\;
\mathrm{clip}(\rho_t,\; 1-\varepsilon,\; 1+\varepsilon)\,\hat{A}_t
\right)
\right]
\]
where $\rho_t = \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) / \pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t \mid s_t)$ is the probability ratio and $\varepsilon = 0.2$ is the clip range.

\subsection{Generalised Advantage Estimation}

Advantages are computed with GAE to balance bias against variance:
\[
\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma \lambda_{\text{GAE}})^l \,\delta_{t+l},
\qquad
\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)
\]

with $\lambda_{\text{GAE}} = 0.95$. Advantages are normalised to zero mean and unit variance before each update to stabilise gradient magnitudes.

\subsection{Training Procedure}

Algorithm~1 describes the full training loop.

\medskip
\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}
\textbf{Algorithm 1: PPO Training for Global Equity Allocation}
\vspace{2pt}
\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Pre-compute \textbf{returns matrix}: for each trade ticker and each date, compute $R_t = P_{t+1}/P_t - 1$ and store in a dictionary for O(1) lookup during training.
    \item Pre-compute \textbf{state cache}: for each date, compute the 6-dimensional \texttt{bmom} state vector and store in memory. This eliminates repeated DataFrame filtering inside the inner loop.
    \item \textbf{For} episode $= 1$ to $N_{\text{ep}} = 50$:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Iterate over all training dates $t = 1, \ldots, T$:
            \begin{enumerate}
                \item Retrieve cached state $s_t$
                \item Sample action $a_t \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_t)$ with \texttt{torch.no\_grad()}
                \item Look up return $R_t$ for chosen ticker; compute reward $r_t = R_t - 0.5\,\sigma_t$
                \item Store transition $(s_t, a_t, r_t, \log\pi_\theta(a_t))$
                \item Every 2,000 steps: run PPO update (4 mini-epochs, batch size 64), then clear buffer
            \end{enumerate}
        \item Log episode reward and Sharpe ratio
    \end{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Return} trained PPO agent
\end{enumerate}
\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}

\section{Implementation Design}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Model Factory Pattern}

The model follows a functional factory pattern used throughout the trading engine. The function \texttt{RLGlobalEquityModel} returns a callable \texttt{run\_model(lf)} that accepts a Polars LazyFrame and returns a weight LazyFrame. This makes it a drop-in replacement for any other model in the registry:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lp{9cm}}
\toprule
Component & Description \\
\midrule
\texttt{PPOAgent}           & Holds policy and value networks, trajectory buffer, and update logic \\
\texttt{train\_rl\_agent}   & Standalone function that trains a \texttt{PPOAgent} on historical data and returns it \\
\texttt{RLGlobalEquityModel}& Factory that wraps a trained agent inside a \texttt{run\_model} closure compatible with the trading engine \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Performance Optimisations}

Two pre-computation steps make the 50-episode training loop feasible on a laptop:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Returns matrix}: All daily returns for all trade tickers are computed once before training begins and stored in a Python dictionary keyed by (ticker, date). Each lookup is O(1) compared to O(n) DataFrame filtering on every step.
    \item \textbf{State cache}: The 6-dimensional blended momentum state for every date is computed once and cached. The inner loop simply indexes into a dictionary rather than constructing features on-the-fly.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Train / Test Registry Separation}

To prevent data leakage, the model creates separate registries and runs entirely separate backtests on the training and test periods:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{train\_models\_registry}: \texttt{RL\_Train} + \texttt{EqualWeight\_Train} evaluated on 2004--2022
    \item \texttt{test\_models\_registry}: \texttt{RL\_Test} + \texttt{EqualWeight\_Test} evaluated on 2022--2025
\end{itemize}

The same trained agent is used for both train and test inference; no re-fitting occurs on test data.

\section{Interpretation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The model learns a \textbf{macro-aware rotation strategy} by mapping cross-asset momentum patterns to regional equity allocations. Representative rules the agent internalises include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Positive commodity \texttt{bmom}} (USO, SLV rising) $\Rightarrow$ favour EWJ-US, EZU-US (export-driven economies)
    \item \textbf{Positive VIXY \texttt{bmom}} (volatility trending higher) $\Rightarrow$ rotate to BIL-US (cash)
    \item \textbf{Positive TLT \texttt{bmom}} (bonds rallying) $\Rightarrow$ risk-off, favour defensive or cash positions
    \item \textbf{Stable or negative VIXY, positive equity momentum} $\Rightarrow$ favour SPY-US
\end{itemize}

Because the \texttt{bmom} signal emphasises the medium-term trend (20- and 40-day windows), these rotation signals are inherently smoother than single-window alternatives, reducing whipsaw and transaction costs.

\section{Validation Methodology}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Out-of-Sample Test Design}

The agent is trained exclusively on data up to September 2022. The three-year test window (September 2022 -- September 2025) covers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A challenging macro environment including the 2022--2023 rate-hiking cycle
    \item The 2023--2024 U.S.\ equity bull market
    \item Elevated cross-asset volatility from geopolitical events
\end{itemize}

This makes the test period a meaningful out-of-sample evaluation of the model's ability to generalise its learned macro intuition.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}

The following metrics are computed for both in-sample and out-of-sample periods:

\[
\text{Annualised Return} = \left(1 + R_{\text{total}}\right)^{252/T} - 1
\]
\[
\text{Annualised Volatility} = \hat{\sigma}_{\text{daily}} \cdot \sqrt{252}
\]
\[
\text{Sharpe Ratio} = \frac{\text{Annualised Return}}{\text{Annualised Volatility}}
\]

\section{How Reinforcement Learning Works}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{The Core Loop}

Reinforcement learning trains an agent through repeated interaction with an environment. At each step the agent observes a state, chooses an action, receives a reward, and updates its policy to make better decisions in the future. The analogy is learning chess through repeated play: the agent discovers which strategies work not from labelled examples but from the consequences of its own decisions.

\subsection{Experience Collection}

For each training episode, the agent simulates the full historical trading sequence:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Observe} the cached macro state $s_t$ (6 \texttt{bmom} features)
    \item \textbf{Choose} an ETF by sampling from $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_t)$
    \item \textbf{Receive} reward $r_t = R_t - 0.5\,\sigma_t$
    \item \textbf{Store} the experience tuple $(s_t, a_t, r_t, \log\pi_\theta(a_t))$
    \item \textbf{Update} the policy every 2,000 steps using the buffered experience
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Policy Updates with PPO}

After collecting a batch of experience, the PPO update proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Compute GAE advantages}: quantify how much better each action was than the value network's expectation.
    \item \textbf{Clip the importance ratio}: prevent the policy from changing so rapidly that the agent overshoots the optimum.
    \item \textbf{Update the actor}: gradient ascent on the clipped objective to increase probability of high-advantage actions.
    \item \textbf{Update the critic}: gradient descent on mean-squared value error to improve future advantage estimates.
\end{enumerate}

The clipping mechanism is the key innovation of PPO. By restricting $\rho_t \in [0.8, 1.2]$, the algorithm avoids the policy collapse that can occur with vanilla policy gradient when a single large update destroys previously learned behaviour.

\subsection{Exploration vs.\ Exploitation}

During training, actions are \emph{sampled} from the policy distribution rather than chosen greedily. Combined with entropy regularisation (coefficient 0.01), this ensures the agent explores diverse allocation strategies early in training. Towards convergence, the distribution sharpens around high-value actions, transitioning from exploration to exploitation naturally.

\subsection{Credit Assignment}

The discount factor $\gamma = 0.95$ encodes intertemporal preference:
\[
G_t = r_t + 0.95\,r_{t+1} + 0.95^2\,r_{t+2} + \cdots
\]
Tomorrow's reward counts for 95\% of today's. This ensures the agent considers the downstream macro consequences of its allocation decisions rather than myopically maximising the next day's return.
